Grace, peace, and mercy from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ.  Amen.

Today is Transfiguration Sunday.  Transfiguration is the day when we celebrate the manifestation of Jesus on the mountain, His face shining like the sun, revealing to the Apostles Peter, James, and John the true nature of Christ, as the Son of God, to whom all should listen.  The glory of that moment remained with the Apostles, as they wrote and spoke of the Christ after His death and resurrection.  God had revealed Himself in the face of Christ.

Our Old Testament lesson too reminds us of the glory of God, for when Moses came down from Mt. Sinai with the two tablets of the law, his face shone. It was a phenomenon that the people of Israel received with a mixture of awe and fear, awe that God had appeared to Moses and transformed his appearance, and fear of the holiness of God that comes with such transformation.  So, in order to keep that image of the glory of God in the face of Moses from unsettling the people of Israel, Moses covered his face with a veil.  Whenever Moses would meet with God, he would remove the veil, and whenever he would return and meet with the people, he would put the veil back over his face.

Such was the glory of the law, because the law was God’s law.  God speaks through His law.  In it, he teaches us what He requires of us.  The law commands us to love God with all our heart, with all our soul, and with all our mind.  It commands us to love our neighbor as ourselves.  The law commands fear, love, and trust in God above all things.  It commands us to treat His name as holy, to call upon Him in every trouble, to hold His Word and preaching as sacred, and to gladly hear and learn it. The law commands us to love, honor, serve, and respect our parents and other authorities, to help and protect our neighbor, to live decent lives of chastity within and without marriage, to defend the reputation of our neighbor against slander, and to be content with our possessions and to help our neighbor keep his own.

In short, the law commands us to be holy, even as He is holy.  The face of Moses conveyed something of this, that holiness wasn’t just for God but for man created in God’s image.  That’s what we are, after all, creatures made in God’s image and meant to share His holiness. But because holiness has been lost by man’s fall into sin, the glory of God in the light of the law becomes a terror.

Like the people of Israel unnerved by the sight of Moses glowing with the glory of God, so you too are unnerved by the demands the law requires of you. When the law is preached in all its sternness, it is terrifying.  It shakes you to your core, because you have not kept it. You have not loved God with all your heart. You have not loved your neighbor as yourself.

So, you either run from God, terrified by His holiness, or like the people of Israel hide God from yourself. You put a veil over the law of God to hide its holy demands from your sight.  Maybe God is not all that serious.  Maybe He is not all that holy.  Perhaps sin is not so bad. Perhaps my gossip, my dishonest gain, my jealous schemes to obtain what rightfully belongs to another, my prayerlessness, my rejection of God’s Word, and my idolatry are not so abhorrent to God.  This is how your sinful heart speaks to itself when it puts a veil over the demands of the law.  Then, in those moments when the veil is removed and you see the law in all its holiness, you once again experience terror…as you should…because God’s holiness is real, and you ask yourself, how shall I escape this holy God and His righteous judgment.

St. Paul says in Second Corinthians that a veil lies over the hearts of Israel.  A veil lies over all people who cannot stand to face the holiness of God, but that veil has been removed in Christ. 

In those moments when the Apostles get a glimpse of the glorified Christ, they too are taken by terror. Hearing God speak from heaven, directing them to hear His Son, the One in whom He is well pleased, they fall to their faces and are terrified.  They are overcome by the sight and sounds of God’s Holiness, but then touched by the comforting hand of Christ who tells them, “Rise, and have no fear.” And in that moment they learn something new about the glory of God.  They learn that the holiness of God is ultimately meant to be a comfort to His people.  We don’t run from God.  And we don’t cover Him to shield ourselves from His holiness.  Christ has taken away the veil and has shown us that God in Christ is a comfort to us. He is a friend to sinners like you. 

As they are coming down from the mountain, Jesus commands His disciples, “Tell no one the vision, until the Son of Man is raised from the dead.” Why this command? Why hide the glory of God they have witnessed and the comforting words of Christ? Why only when the Son of Man is raised from the dead? What is all this business about raising from the dead? And why in the world, when Christ has been glorified in this way, are we talking about death?

The Mount of Transfiguration, which conveys wonderfully the glory Christ is not a stand alone event. The comforting touch of Christ transcends the mount of Transfiguration and descends to Jerusalem, to the anguish of the Mount of Olives, to the court of Pilate, and to the horror of Golgotha. The comfort of, “rise and have no fear,” Is bundled with the anguished cries of our Savior on the cross as he thirsts, as He cries out, “My God, My God, why have you forsaken Me,” as He triumphantly declares, “It is finished.” And it all is woven into a seamless whole, beginning with the veil of Moses and ending with the veil of the temple being torn in two.  The judgment that we fear, that we hide ourselves from, and the terror of the law that we instinctively hang a veil over, has been removed.  Ultimately, the glory of God is not experienced in the law at all, but in the mercy that God has shown by laying all your sins on Christ. Do not be afraid. God’s judgment has passed over you in the flesh of Christ.  The terror of the law has been nailed to the cross, and you have peace with God. The glory of God is in the wounds of Christ. The veil of Moses has been torn in two.

Having died and rose again, God’s beloved Son has rescued you from God’s righteous wrath.  The sinless Christ has taken your sin, and He has triumphed over sin, death, hell, and the grave.  And now He comes to touch you. His hand is extended to you in your Baptism, where God united you to Christ’s life, death, and resurrection, where He buried you with Christ, and then raised you again with Him, where He tells you, “Rise, and have no fear.” As often as you feel the terror of the law, come again to your Baptism, remember the promises He has made you there, and cling to God’s Word in it.  Repent and believe the Good News. 

And the comforting touch of Christ is being extended from the right hand of God through the Sacrament of the Altar. Here the very flesh of Christ and His shed blood is given to you to eat and drink. Here, by giving you His body and blood, Christ tells you, “Rise, and have no fear.”

Come now.  The curse of sin and the terror of the law has been put away.  God comforts you in the face of the crucified One and tells you He is not your enemy but your friend, and you need not hide yourself from His friendship. Amen.
